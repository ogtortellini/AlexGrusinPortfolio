Restomod 3000GT VR4 Project

The Mitsubishi 3000GT VR4 Rebuild Project is an ongoing technical and educational vehicle restoration effort led by mechanical engineering student Alex Grusin. Spanning multiple years, the project chronicles the complete mechanical and electrical revitalization of a 1997 Mitsubishi 3000GT VR4, evolving from a rusted, non-functional platform into a high-performance, street-capable machine. Initiated in 2018, the build has included full chassis and subframe restoration, two engine rebuilds, a transmission overhaul, and a highly iterative ECU tuning process. The project serves as a hands-on laboratory for learning systems integration, reliability engineering, and motorsport-grade calibration.

1. Introduction

In June 2018, at age 14, Alex Grusin began the project while visiting family. Initial repairs occurred at his uncle's house, where he and his father fixed a leaking rear brake line and restored AC functionality. Despite limited experience, Alex absorbed foundational mechanical skills during these early repairs.

Afterward, Alex and his father drove the car 600 miles home. The car was in severe disrepair: no air conditioning, holes through the floor, and only a temporary brake line fix. That summer, Alex initiated a full teardown of the undercarriage. Severely rusted floor sections were cut out and replaced with junkyard donor panels, which were welded in. Both front and rear subframes were removed, stripped, refinished, and reassembled with polyurethane bushings, new axles, fresh seals, and rubber lines. He also rebuilt all four brake calipers and installed new e-brake shoes inside the rear rotors. During this first year, only basic engine maintenance was performed (seals, timing belt, water pump). The engine internals were untouched.

2. Project Timeline

2.1 Daily Driving and Manual Mastery (2019)

By September 2019, the car was roadworthy. Alex taught himself to drive manual in it and began using the 3000GT as his daily driver. The vehicle remained mechanically stable through mid-2020 while Alex gained real-world experience.

2.2 Transmission Rebuild (2020)

By fall 2020, driveability issues led Alex to initiate a full teardown of the W6MG1 6-speed manual transmission. Symptoms included grinding into 3rd gear, difficulty holding reverse, and hard engagement of 1st. The teardown revealed:

  • Severely worn synchro friction surfaces on 3rd and reverse
  • Broken roll pin on the 3/4 shift fork
  • Wear on 1st/2nd gear teeth and sliders

The rebuild included:

  • New 3/4 and reverse hub/slider assemblies
  • Replacement of input and intermediate shaft bearings
  • New seals, shift detents, and stabilizer bushings
  • Refreshed linkage and bushings to reduce slop
  • Consideration of carbon synchros vs. OEM (ultimately not used due to cost/performance tradeoffs)

Reassembly was completed by late 2020. This marked Alex’s first full experience with gearbox internals, helping him understand precision engagement geometry and wear behavior.

2.3 First Engine Failure and OEM Rebuild (Nov 2020 – Sept 2021)

In November 2020, the factory engine failed catastrophically. At that point, it had never been internally rebuilt. Between November 2020 and April 2021, Alex and his dad carefully evaluated options. They considered a forged performance rebuild featuring Ross pistons and high-compression gaskets, but ultimately opted for an OEM-spec configuration due to cost, complexity, and intended use.

The rebuild included:

  • Cleaned and honed block (MMW)
  • Polished OEM crankshaft
  • Stock pistons and rods
  • Full OEM gasket kit including MLS-style Athena head gaskets
  • Refreshed oil pump, water pump, timing components
  • Verified ring gaps, bearing clearances using plastigage and micrometers
  • Carefully torqued and staged ARP main studs

Assembly began April 2021 and was completed in September. The first start occurred that month, marking a key mechanical and emotional milestone in the project.

2.4 ECU Tuning Phase 1 (Jan–Feb 2022)

With the rebuilt engine complete, Alex began tuning the car using a Chrome ECU, Tactrix OpenPort 2.0 cable, and wideband O2 sensor. Tuning was supported by Morgan of RacerX Performance. The calibration process involved:

Files 001–008 – Lucas 550cc Injector Tuning:

  • 001: Initial scale and latency values
  • 002: Adjusted latency, still too rich
  • 003: Applied manufacturer latencies
  • 004: Reduced injector scaling by 10%
  • 005: Increased latency +10%
  • 006: Increased latency +20%
  • 007: Increased latency +30%
  • 008: Reduced scaling again by another 10%

Files 009–015 – VE Table and Timing Work:

  • 009–010: VE smoothing for mid-load operation
  • 011: Introduced -5° timing retard at 80%+ load, blended down to 60%
  • 012–013: VE tuning under boost conditions (WGDC disabled), with conservative spark
  • 014: Advanced timing +5° from the retard baseline
  • 015: Retreated timing to +3° for knock margin

Files 016–026 – FIC 850cc Transition and Knock Mitigation:

  • 016: Initial FIC injector calibration, new scaling and latency
  • 017: +14% scale increase
  • 018: -14% latency reduction
  • 019: +5% latency test
  • 020: VE adjusted for +3% latency
  • 021: Reversal test, -3% latency
  • 022: Locked-in VE and timing with -5° base
  • 023: +5° cumulative MBT test
  • 024: +3° MBT test
  • 025: +2° MBT test
  • 026: -2° in knock-prone RPM/load zones

Tuning Phase 1 was executed with wastegate duty cycle fully disabled to isolate fueling and ignition behavior.

3. Challenges & Learnings

The 3000GT is in a near-complete state mechanically and electronically. With multiple layers of tuning and hardware validation behind it, the car is poised for track shakedown and continued refinement. Alex’s effort has transformed a rusted-out shell into a technically robust performance platform. Every component—from welds to wiring—was shaped by hand and guided by data. The project stands as a meticulous, transparent chronicle of mechanical development, learning, and growth.
