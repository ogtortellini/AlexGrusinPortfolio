Hydrolysis Power Cell for Space Applications

Developing a proof-of-concept system for generating hydrogen and oxygen from water ice for ISRU (In-Situ Resource Utilization) on Mars.

Introduction

This project, undertaken for a senior design course, focused on the conceptualization and partial prototyping of a hydrolysis power cell system. The primary objective was to demonstrate the feasibility of extracting water ice (simulated), melting it, and then electrolyzing the water to produce hydrogen (for fuel) and oxygen (for life support or oxidizer) in a simulated Martian environment. This aligns with NASA's goals for In-Situ Resource Utilization (ISRU) to reduce mission mass and cost.

Problem Statement

Future long-duration missions to Mars will require sustainable resources. Transporting all necessary water, fuel, and oxygen from Earth is prohibitively expensive and logistically complex. The presence of water ice on Mars offers an opportunity to produce these vital resources on-site. This project addresses the challenge of designing a compact, efficient system to convert Martian water ice into usable hydrogen and oxygen.

System Design Overview

Our proposed system consists of several key stages:

  1. Ice Acquisition & Delivery: (Conceptual) A mechanism to drill/collect ice and deliver it to the processing unit.
  2. Melting Chamber: An insulated chamber to melt the water ice efficiently using minimal power.
  3. Water Filtration/Purification: (Conceptual) A system to remove impurities from the melted water before electrolysis.
  4. Electrolysis Cell: A Proton Exchange Membrane (PEM) electrolyzer to split water into hydrogen and oxygen.
  5. Gas Storage & Management: Separate storage tanks for hydrogen and oxygen, with pressure and temperature monitoring.
  6. Power System: (Conceptual) Solar panels and batteries to power the entire system.
  7. Control System: Microcontroller-based system to manage the process, monitor sensors, and control actuators.

Focus on Key Systems: Melting & Electrolysis

Due to time and resource constraints, our physical prototype focused on the melting chamber and the electrolysis process. We designed and built a small-scale melting unit and integrated it with a commercially available PEM electrolysis cell. My role involved CAD design of the melting chamber, selection of the electrolyzer, and integration of temperature sensors and heating elements.

Thermal Analysis & Power Estimation

A significant portion of the project involved theoretical analysis. We performed thermal simulations for the melting chamber to optimize insulation and heating element placement. Power consumption estimates were calculated for each stage of the process to size the conceptual solar array and battery storage.

Prototype Construction & Testing

The prototype successfully demonstrated the melting of simulated water ice (regular ice mixed with some fine sand to simulate regolith) and subsequent electrolysis to produce measurable amounts of hydrogen and oxygen. Testing was conducted at atmospheric pressure due to limitations in creating a vacuum chamber for a full Martian environment simulation.

Results & Future Work

The project confirmed the basic viability of the core concept. The prototype produced gases at expected rates for the given electrolyzer specifications. Future work would involve:

  • Scaling up the system for higher production rates.
  • Integrating a water purification stage.
  • Developing a more sophisticated control system with automation.
  • Testing in a vacuum chamber to better simulate Martian atmospheric conditions.
  • Designing and incorporating the ice acquisition and delivery mechanism.
