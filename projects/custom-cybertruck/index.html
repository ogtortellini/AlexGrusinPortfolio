Custom Cybertruck Project

A deep dive into the design and development of a unique Cybertruck concept aimed at being "as Texas as possible."

1. Introduction

This project chronicles the journey of transforming a Tesla Cybertruck into a statement piece, commissioned by a client to advertise their virtual power plant business in Texas. The core request was to make the Cybertruck "as Texas as possible," leading to a unique design exploration that balanced bold aesthetics with practical modifications. Initially exploring custom sheet metal body panels, the project evolved towards a distinctive tubular exoskeleton-inspired design, focusing on altering the truck's proportions and enhancing its utility and visual impact. This page details the design iterations, fabrication choices, and the collaborative process that brought this one-of-a-kind Cybertruck to life.

2. Project Goals & Motivation

The primary motivation for this project was to fulfill a client's vision for a highly customized Cybertruck that would serve as a mobile advertisement for their virtual power plant business (which assists homeowners with Texas's VPP program and installs free home battery backups) and embody a rugged, "Texas" spirit. Key objectives included:

  • Client Vision: To create a Cybertruck that is unmistakably unique and "as Texas as possible."
  • Aesthetic Transformation: To significantly alter the Cybertruck's stock bodylines and proportions, particularly addressing the controversial tapering side profile.
  • Functional Enhancements: To incorporate practical elements like a robust roof rack, tool/gear mounting solutions (molle panels), a winch-ready front bumper, and a durable rear bash bar.
  • Design Exploration: To investigate various customization approaches, from custom sheet metal panels to a full tubular exoskeleton, ultimately refining these ideas into a cohesive and buildable design.
  • Showcasing Makerspace Capabilities: To leverage the team's expertise in CAD design, metal fabrication (cutting, bending, welding), and custom part creation.

3. Design Process

The design process was highly iterative, involving close collaboration with the client to translate their "Texas as possible" vision into a tangible and striking Cybertruck modification. Our team at the makerspace leveraged its fabrication expertise to guide design choices.

3.1 Initial Concepts & Ideation

The initial brainstorming sessions were broad, with ideas ranging from the audacious (nearly mounting a longhorn skull to the hood) to more conventional modifications. We first proposed redesigning body panels using laser-cut and bent stainless steel sheets to alter the truck's bodylines. However, this approach was deemed not cost-effective and didn't offer a significant enough departure from the OEM design language to meet the client's desire for a truly unique vehicle.

3.2 CAD Modeling & Iteration

Given our team's familiarity with tube cutting and welding, we pivoted to the concept of a tubular exoskeleton. This offered greater design freedom and the potential for a more radical transformation. Using Fusion 360, I dedicated over 100 hours to developing three distinct versions of the Cybertruck, exploring different levels of exoskeleton integration and aesthetic styles. One early concept is shown in the "Initial Exoskeleton Sketch." The "Final Proposal CAD" image reflects the design presented to the client. These designs were presented to the client, and through several collaborative meetings, we refined the vision. Ultimately, a full exoskeleton was deemed too complex, leading us to a hybrid approach. We selectively incorporated key elements from the exoskeleton concepts, such as the roof rack and support structures, to achieve the desired visual impact and functionality. The front bumper design was validated for strength using FEA, as seen in the "Front Bumper Static Test (Bodyweight)" image.

3.3 Material Selection & Finishing

The primary material chosen for the custom additions was mild steel tubing (1/8 inch wall thickness). Specifically, 2-inch Outer Diameter (OD) tubing was used for the front and rear bumpers due to strength requirements, while 1-inch OD tubing was selected for the roof rack and ladder for a balance of strength and weight. All custom steel components underwent a thorough finishing process: they were sandblasted to prepare the surface and then powder-coated for durability and a high-quality aesthetic before final installation. The molle panels were custom fiber-lasered.

3.4 Wheel & Tire Selection

Achieving the perfect stance and off-road capability required careful consideration of wheel and tire fitment. A significant amount of research and calculation went into selecting options that would complement the Cybertruck's modified aesthetics and performance goals. We analyzed how different wheel sizes and offsets would affect the "poke" – how far the wheels extend from the fenders – compared to the stock setup (55.8mm stock poke).

Several options, primarily featuring Black Rhino Armory wheels, were presented to the client:

  • Option 1: 18x9.5 wheels with a -18mm offset, fitted with 35-inch tires. This setup resulted in a poke of 138.65mm (an increase of 82.85mm over stock). The net cost for this package was estimated around $2,295.45 after considering the sale of old wheels.
  • Option 2: 18x9.5 wheels, also with a -18mm offset, but with larger 37-inch tires. This maintained the same poke as Option 1 but offered increased tire height, with an estimated net cost of $2,575.45.
  • Option 3: 20x9.5 wheels with a +6mm offset, also fitted with 37-inch tires. This option provided a poke of 114.65mm (an increase of 58.85mm over stock), offering a slightly more tucked-in look compared to the -18mm offset options. The estimated net cost for this premium setup was $3,095.45.

These costs included the wheel and tire package, a spare, and associated expenses like lift rods, ceramic coating, lug nuts, and hub rings, offset by the potential resale value of the original wheels and tires. This detailed breakdown allowed the client to make an informed decision based on aesthetics, performance, and budget.

4. Key Features & Innovations

The final design incorporates several custom-fabricated elements that collectively give the Cybertruck a unique, aggressive, and functional "exoskeleton" look:

Elevated Roof Rack & Tailgate-to-Rack Support Struts

A prominent 1-inch OD mild steel tube roof rack, originating from the full exoskeleton concepts, drastically shifts up the tapering bodyline, correcting the Cybertruck's side profile proportions. Robust struts connect the tailgate area to the roof rack, enhancing structural rigidity and contributing to the exoskeleton aesthetic.

Custom Molle Panels & Side Access Ladder

Fiber-lasered molle panels are integrated with the support struts, offering versatile mounting points for tools and gear. A 1-inch OD mild steel tube ladder, mounted from the roof rack to the side steps, improves accessibility.

Winch-Ready Front Bumper & Mad-Max Style Rear Bash Bar

A simplified yet strong 2-inch OD mild steel tube front bumper was designed for a winch. A distinctive 2-inch OD tubular rear bash bar, mounting to the frame and tow hitch, provides robust protection and an aggressive look.

Cohesive Exoskeleton Appearance

While not a full, interconnected exoskeleton, the strategic placement and design of these powder-coated tubular components successfully create the visual impression of one, fulfilling the client's "Texas as possible" vision.

5. Challenges & Solutions

Translating the client's abstract "as Texas as possible" request into concrete design features while balancing aesthetics, functionality, and a $12,000 budget was an ongoing challenge. The initial pivot from costly sheet metal to more familiar mild steel tubing was a key early solution.

Tube Bending Difficulties

A significant hurdle emerged with tube bending. Our initial plan to retrofit a high-quality manual bender frame with a hydraulic ram from Harbor Freight hit a snag with the bending dies. Professional dies are expensive, and our attempts to create our own using our plasma cutter and 3D printers were ultimately unsuccessful. The custom dies caused kinking and marring of the tubes. This entire endeavor cost us more than a month.

Fabrication Precision

Fabrication precision was another critical challenge. For the rear bumper, we built a jig and relied on engineering drawings. The roof rack, being the largest and most complex assembly, demanded the most refinement. This necessitated multiple cycles of cutting, adjusting, and re-welding.

Full Vehicle Vinyl Wrap

The project also included a full vehicle vinyl wrap. Taking a week-long "break" from metal fabrication, two of us (with occasional help) undertook this task. It was my first experience wrapping an entire vehicle, and it proved to be a steep learning curve.

7. Outcome & Learnings

The custom Cybertruck project is now complete, and the vehicle is finished and on the road. It successfully fulfills the client's vision, serving as a unique promotional vehicle that is fully functional for off-road use and undeniably "turns heads everywhere it goes." The project underscored the importance of iterative design (with over 100 hours in CAD alone), the necessity of clear client communication, and the strategic advantage of leveraging existing team strengths. The transition from complex CAD models to a physically manufactured product, especially with fabrication challenges like tube bending and achieving precision on large welded assemblies like the roof rack, provided invaluable hands-on experience. The first-time experience with a full vehicle vinyl wrap also added a new skillset and appreciation for that craft.
