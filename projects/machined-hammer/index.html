Machined Hammer Project

A hands-on machining project creating a functional steel hammer.

Introduction

This project was part of an introductory machining course, focusing on developing fundamental skills in manual milling and lathing. The objective was to design and fabricate a small, functional hammer from raw steel stock, adhering to specified tolerances and design constraints.

Design Phase

The design process involved creating 2D drawings for the hammer head and handle. Considerations included ergonomics, balance, and the intended use of the hammer. The head was designed with a flat striking face and a peen on the opposite side. The handle was designed for a comfortable grip and secure attachment to the head.

Fabrication Process

The hammer head was machined from a block of mild steel using a manual milling machine for shaping and a lathe for creating the hole for the handle. The handle was turned from a cylindrical steel rod on a manual lathe, including threading for attachment to the head.

  • Facing and squaring stock material.
  • Milling contours and angles for the hammer head.
  • Drilling and boring the eye of the hammer head.
  • Turning the handle to specified diameters.
  • Knurling the grip area on the handle.
  • Single-point threading on the lathe for the handle and tapping the head.
  • Finishing and deburring all components.

Outcome & Skills Gained

The project resulted in a functional, well-crafted hammer that met all design specifications. This hands-on experience was invaluable in developing proficiency with manual machine tools, understanding material properties, and appreciating the importance of precision in manufacturing. Key skills enhanced include blueprint reading, machine setup, various milling and lathing operations, and quality control.
